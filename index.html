Unit 1: The Core of Redox Reactions

At its heart, electrochemistry is the study of chemical reactions that involve the transfer of electrons. These are called Redox (reduction-oxidation) reactions.

Mnemonic Device: OIL RIG

Oxidation Is Loss of electrons.
Reduction Is Gain of electrons.

To identify if a redox reaction is taking place, chemists assign Oxidation States (or numbers) to every atom in a compound. An oxidation state is the hypothetical charge an atom would have if all bonds to different atoms were 100% ionic.

Fundamental Rules for Oxidation Numbers:

1. Elements in their elemental state (e.g., O₂, Fe, Na) are always 0.
2. Monatomic ions (e.g., Na⁺, Cl⁻) match their ionic charge (+1, -1).
3. Oxygen in compounds is usually -2 (except in peroxides where it is -1).
4. Hydrogen is +1 when bonded to nonmetals and -1 when bonded to metals.

Unit 2: Galvanic (Voltaic) Cells

A Galvanic Cell uses a spontaneous chemical reaction to generate electrical energy. It splits a redox reaction into two separate chambers (half-cells) connected by a wire and a salt bridge.

AN OX and RED CAT

Anode is the site of Oxidation.
Reduction occurs at the Cathode.

Electrons flow spontaneously through the external circuit from the Anode (-) to the Cathode (+). This flow occurs because one metal has a greater tendency to lose electrons (higher oxidation potential) than the other.

Standard Cell Potential ($E^0_{cell}$)

The standard cell potential is the voltage generated by a cell under standard conditions (1 M concentrations, 1 atm pressure, 25°C). It is calculated as:

E⁰_cell = E⁰_cathode - E⁰_anode

A positive $E^0_{cell}$ indicates the reaction is spontaneous (Galvanic). A salt bridge containing inert ions (like K⁺ and NO₃⁻) is vital to balance accumulating charge as reactions progress.

Unit 3: Electrolysis & Electroplating

An Electrolytic Cell uses electrical energy from an external power supply to drive a non-spontaneous chemical reaction. Unlike Galvanic cells, the cell potential is negative ($E^0_{cell} < 0$).

A classic industrial application is Electroplating, where a thin layer of a precious metal (like Gold, Silver, or Copper) is deposited onto another metal object (like a key or spoon) using electrolysis.

Faraday's First Law of Electrolysis

The mass ($m$) of a substance deposited on an electrode is directly proportional to the total electrical charge ($Q = I \times t$) passed through the solution.

Quantifying Plating: Faraday's Equation

To calculate the mass of metal deposited, we use:

m = (I • t • M) / (z • F)

Where:
• I = Current in Amperes (A)
• t = Time in seconds (s)
• M = Molar Mass of the metal (g/mol)
• z = Number of electrons transferred per metal ion (valence)
• F = Faraday's Constant (96,485 Coulombs/mol e⁻)

Unit 4: The Nernst Equation

So far, we have only looked at standard cell potentials. However, cell potentials change when we alter concentrations or temperature. To calculate cell potentials under non-standard conditions, we use the Nernst Equation.

Named after Walther Nernst, this equation connects thermodynamics to electrochemistry:

E = E⁰ - (R • T) / (n • F) • ln(Q)

At 298.15 K (25°C), we can convert to base-10 log and simplify the constants:

E = E⁰ - (0.0592 / n) • log₁₀(Q)

Where:
• E = Cell Potential under non-standard conditions
• E⁰ = Standard Cell Potential
• n = Moles of electrons transferred
• Q = Reaction Quotient ($[\text{Products}] / [\text{Reactants}]$)

Concentration Cells

A cell can be constructed using the same metal for both electrodes, differing only in concentrations. E.g., Ag (0.01M) and Ag (1.0M). The potential is driven entirely by entropy: ions move to equalize concentration.
